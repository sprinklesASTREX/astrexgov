Nexus I Orbit And Engine Test Run Succeeds As Ambitions For Launch In May Increase.

Astrex engineers say the latest Nexus I orbit and engine test run delivered stable performance, increasing confidence around the May 24 launch target.

Astrex News Desk Kennedy Space Center, Florida Updated April 15, 2026

Nexus I completed an orbit and engine test run this week, giving Astrex teams one of their strongest signs yet that the vehicle is moving closer to flight readiness. The test focused on engine response, ascent timing, stage behavior, and the thin-rocket control profile planned for the first launch.

Early review data showed clean ignition behavior, steady thrust output, and a controlled guidance sequence through the simulated climb. Engineers also confirmed that the vehicle maintained stable attitude during the orbit-insertion portion of the test, an important milestone for the satellite deployment mission.

May Launch Confidence Builds

The successful run has increased internal confidence around the May 24 launch plan, though Astrex leadership is still keeping June available as a backup window. Teams at Kennedy Space Center are expected to use the new data during the next flight readiness meeting.

The engine team is now reviewing fuel flow, chamber pressure, and shutdown timing. The orbit team is studying pitch control, staging margins, and the simulated deployment path for the DOJ military satellite package.

Final Tests Still Ahead

Astrex still has pad rehearsals, crew procedure checks, range coordination, and one more mission replay review before the countdown can be cleared. The company says the goal is not just to launch Nexus I, but to prove the thin-rocket design can operate cleanly through ascent and orbit.

If the next reviews hold, Nexus I will remain pointed toward a May launch attempt from Kennedy Space Center, with public mission coverage expected through Astrex Streaming.

CEO Kaylee Harper Believes Nexus I Will Launch In June Despite Plans On May 24th.

Astrex leadership says Kennedy Space Center remains the launch site, but range conditions, vehicle checks, and crew readiness reviews could move the first Nexus I flight window.

Astrex News Desk Kennedy Space Center, Florida Updated April 15, 2026

Astrex CEO Kaylee Harper said the Nexus I team is still preparing for the planned May 24 launch, but she believes a June launch is becoming more realistic as final checks continue at Kennedy Space Center.

The mission is designed as a crewed flight that will deploy DOJ military satellites and test how thin rockets behave in orbit. Harper said the company wants the first Nexus flight to be clean, controlled, and fully ready before the countdown leaves the pad.

Conditions Under Review

Astrex teams are watching upper-atmosphere winds, lightning limits, launch range timing, pad cooling schedules, and final guidance checks. A thermal reading in the upper-stage avionics bay also triggered an extra review cycle.

Engineers are also running more thin-body stress simulations. Because Nexus I is testing a slim rocket profile, the flight team wants extra confidence that the vehicle will remain stable through ascent, staging, and orbital insertion.

A crew walk-through is scheduled before the next flight readiness meeting, with satellite deployment timing and return procedures listed as major agenda items.

Kennedy Space Center Remains The Pad

Astrex says there are no plans to move the mission away from Kennedy Space Center. Ground crews are continuing pad rehearsals, satellite integration checks, crew procedures, and emergency landing protocol reviews.

If the May 24 attempt holds, Nexus I will lift off at 5:30 PM Eastern. If the team slips the mission, June would give Astrex more time to review weather patterns, crew readiness, and the DOJ satellite deployment sequence.

Harper said the company will not rush the launch for the calendar alone. The final call is expected after the next full launch rehearsal and a range coordination briefing.

Astrex also confirmed that streaming plans are being prepared for public mission coverage, including launch footage, rehearsal clips, and orbit test replays as they become available.
